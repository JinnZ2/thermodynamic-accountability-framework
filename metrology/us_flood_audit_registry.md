# US Flood Metrological Audit Registry

CC0. Living document. Append-only -- never delete past entries.

## Purpose

Document the metrology problem in US flood measurement, 1850-present.

**Floods are the worst case for a different reason than drought.**
Drought has competing index methodologies. Floods have **causal
inversion** -- the data is polluted at the assumption layer, not just
the measurement layer.

The institutional flood narrative attributes the apparent flood
increase to climate change. The actual causal chain is:

1. Remove wetlands and riparian buffers (1900-2000 zoning)
2. Pave floodplains (1950-2024 urban development)
3. Channelize streams (1900-1980 Army Corps)
4. Strip permeable land cover (continuous)
5. Build in floodplains (continuous, accelerating)
6. **Water does what water has always done**
7. Call the resulting damage "unprecedented climate event"
8. Propose climate solutions (carbon reduction)
9. Continue removing the hydrological infrastructure that worked

This is not a measurement problem. It is a **framework problem**.
The data is being collected to answer a question
("is climate change causing more floods?") that is the wrong
question. The right question is: "of the observed damage trend,
how much is precipitation change, how much is land-use change,
and how much is infrastructure placement?"

Most institutional flood analyses do not separate these. They count
disasters and attribute the trend to climate.

This registry exists to surface the assumption layer alongside the
measurement layer.

---

## The two-layer corruption

Every flood claim in the institutional literature inherits two layers
of methodology bias:

### Layer 1: Measurement drift (the standard metrology problem)

- Stream gauge network changing density over time
- Methodology revisions (FEMA Flood Insurance Rate Maps redrawn
  every few years)
- Instrument changes (manual gauges -> telemetered -> satellite)
- Reanalysis products replacing direct observations

### Layer 2: Causal inversion (the assumption bias)

This is the deeper problem. The framework assumes:

- Flood = excess water in places it should not be
- Therefore: more flood = more water = more rain = climate change

The framework does not assume:

- Flood = water meeting impervious surface where infiltration was removed
- Therefore: more flood = less infiltration capacity = land-use choice

Both framings are physically valid. The institutional literature
chose the first. Any data collected within that framework cannot
answer questions the framework excluded by definition.

---

## The technology pinnacle assumption

The deeper bias underneath the causal inversion: **the assumption
that current technology is the reference standard against which
historical practice is judged.**

This shows up everywhere in flood data interpretation:

- Wood roads in boreal wetlands -> "primitive infrastructure"
- Riparian floodplains -> "underdeveloped land"
- Wetlands -> "wasted real estate" (literally -- "reclamation projects")
- Meandering streams -> "inefficient drainage"
- Permeable surfaces -> "obsolete construction"

When you then measure floods in landscapes that have been "improved"
by these assumptions, you are measuring **the failure mode of the
assumptions themselves**. Calling that failure mode "climate change"
masks the choice that caused it.

The substrate-primary frame inverts this:

- Wood roads in boreal wetlands -> **observation-based engineering**
- Riparian floodplains -> **water-management infrastructure**
- Wetlands -> **flood control assets** (free, self-maintaining)
- Meandering streams -> **energy dissipation systems**
- Permeable surfaces -> **infiltration capacity**

In this frame, "more flooding in modern era" is the **expected
result of removing the infrastructure that managed water**.

## Measurement Eras

### Era F1: Pre-gauge era (1850-1900)

- **Primary records:** newspapers, county records, ferry/bridge logs
- **Detection:** required population near flooding river
- **Methodology:** "high water marks" on buildings, oral history
- **Coverage:** river towns documented; rural floods invisible
- **Land use:** mostly intact wetlands, native riparian forest,
  meandering streams
- **Known biases:**
  - Most "flood damage" claims describe events that today would
    be considered routine high water -- but caused damage in 1880
    because levees were primitive and rebuilding was hard
  - Indigenous knowledge of seasonal flood patterns excluded
  - Some "floods" in records were actually normal seasonal
    inundation that built soil fertility (pre-Army Corps thinking)
- **What survives:** general impression of "flood years" in
  populated river basins
- **Crucial baseline:** this is the only era where land cover was
  near-natural across most of CONUS. Any flood data from this
  era reflects what water does on intact landscapes.

### Era F2: Early streamgage era (1889-1930)

- **Primary records:** USGS streamgage network founded 1889;
  first gauge at Embudo, NM
- **Coverage:** ~1,500 gauges by 1930, mostly on major rivers
- **Methodology:** staff gauges, manual readings, weekly to daily
- **Land use:** rapid wetland drainage (Federal Swamp Land Acts
  1849-1860 transferred 64M acres to states for "reclamation");
  Mississippi levees expanding; railroad/road network paving begins
- **Known biases:**
  - Gauges placed where economically important (river commerce)
  - Tributaries and small watersheds unmonitored
  - "Flood frequency" calculations based on <30 year records ->
    statistically meaningless extrapolation to "100-year flood"
  - Wetland loss ALREADY ~50% of pre-settlement extent by 1900
- **What this means:** the "baseline" used by modern flood
  insurance maps comes from data collected on **already-degraded
  landscapes**. The 100-year flood threshold is calibrated to a
  landscape with reduced infiltration capacity from day one.

### Era F3: Army Corps channelization era (1930-1965)

- **Primary records:** USGS gauges (~3,500 by 1950, 7,000 by 1965)
- **Major land-use change:**
  - 1928 Mississippi River flood -> Flood Control Act 1936
  - Massive Army Corps channelization (1936-1980)
  - Federal Highway Act 1956 -> 41,000 miles of impervious surface
  - Wetland loss reaches ~75% of pre-settlement extent
- **Methodology:**
  - Telemetered gauges introduced
  - "Flood frequency analysis" (Bulletin 17, 1967) standardizes
    statistical methods
  - Flood Insurance Rate Maps (FIRMs) begin
- **Known biases:**
  - Channel "improvements" REMOVE meandering, INCREASE peak flow
  - Gauges measure straightened channels, not natural ones
  - Pre-channelization hydrology is unrecoverable from gauge data
- **What survives:** quantitative streamflow data, but on a
  landscape progressively engineered for faster drainage.
- **Crucial pollution:** the apparent INCREASE in peak flows
  during this era is largely the engineered consequence of
  removing flood-attenuation infrastructure (wetlands, meanders).

### Era F4: Suburbanization era (1965-1990)

- **Primary records:** USGS gauge network peaks at ~7,500
- **Major land-use change:**
  - Suburban sprawl explodes
  - Impervious surface fraction in metro areas reaches 30-50%
  - Stormwater systems concentrate runoff into single outfalls
  - Floodplain development continues (insurance subsidies make it
    economically rational)
- **Methodology:**
  - Doppler radar precipitation estimates introduced
  - FIRM revisions become routine
  - "100-year flood" definition formalized in NFIP regulations
- **Known biases:**
  - Same precipitation event causes larger flood peaks in
    developed watersheds -- but this is attributed to "more rain"
    rather than less infiltration
  - FIRM maps redrawn -> properties move in/out of flood zones
    based on regulatory choices, not physical changes
  - National Flood Insurance Program creates moral hazard
    (subsidized rebuilding in flood zones)

### Era F5: Climate-attribution era (1990-2010)

- **Primary records:** USGS gauge network DECLINING from peak
  (budget cuts close ~1,500 gauges)
- **Methodology:**
  - Climate change attribution becomes mainstream
  - Attribution studies use precipitation-based indices
  - Precipitation extremes increasingly used as "flood proxy"
- **Known biases:**
  - **Gauge network shrinking** while flood claims increase ->
    sampling bias toward already-flooded areas
  - "Extreme precipitation" trends conflated with flood trends,
    even though land-use changes amplify the conversion of
    precipitation to peak flow
  - FEMA disaster declarations become primary "flood frequency"
    proxy in popular literature
- **Critical observation:** the precipitation trend in this era
  is genuinely upward in some regions (real climate signal). But
  the flood damage trend is much steeper than precipitation alone
  predicts. The gap between the two is land-use change.

### Era F6: Satellite + ML era (2010-present)

- **Primary records:** GRACE/GRACE-FO satellite + SMAP soil
  moisture + radar precipitation + remaining USGS gauges
- **Major changes:**
  - First chance at "true" flood-relevant measurement
    (soil moisture from space, mass anomalies from gravity)
  - But satellite era is too short to establish trend
  - Land use changes continue (urban sprawl, wetland loss)
- **Known biases:**
  - SMAP soil moisture only since 2015 -> 9 years of data
  - GRACE data gap 2017-2018
  - All trend claims in this era are confounded by extremely
    short observational record

## The land-use baseline that's been erased

Pre-settlement North America had hydrology infrastructure that
modern engineering removed:

| System                  | Pre-1850 extent | 2024 extent           | Function lost                   |
|-------------------------|-----------------|-----------------------|---------------------------------|
| Wetlands (lower 48)     | ~221M acres     | ~107M acres           | Flood attenuation, infiltration |
| Beaver dams (CONUS)     | ~250M dams      | ~10M dams             | Watershed-scale water retention |
| Riparian forest         | ~60M acres      | ~23M acres            | Floodplain energy dissipation   |
| Meandering streams      | most channels   | <30% of stream miles  | Velocity reduction              |
| Permeable urban surface | ~100%           | <50% in metros        | Infiltration capacity           |
| Floodplain forest       | ~25M acres      | ~6M acres             | Storage during peak flow        |

The combined effect: a 5-10x reduction in landscape water-holding
capacity at watershed scale.

When you then measure "flood frequency" on a landscape with 5-10x
less buffering capacity than the pre-settlement baseline, the
apparent increase reflects the lost infrastructure, not changed
precipitation.

## The wood road observation

Specific case: boreal wetland transportation.

**Pre-1950 practice (substrate-primary):**

- Logs laid as roadway, replaced as trees fall
- Surface flexes with frost heave (no cracking)
- Water passes through gaps (no pooling, no erosion)
- Permafrost preserved (no deep heat penetration)
- Self-repairing (every windfall provides new road material)
- Cost: zero ongoing maintenance budget

**Post-1950 practice (technology-pinnacle):**

- Asphalt/concrete on engineered base
- Surface cracks under frost heave
- Water pools, erodes shoulders, creates ice in winter
- Permafrost thaws (heat conducts through pavement)
- Requires constant repair
- Cost: continuous capital + maintenance budget

The wood road wasn't "primitive infrastructure inferior to modern
methods." It was **observation-based engineering responsive to
the actual constraints of the substrate**. The "modern" replacement
ignores those constraints, requires perpetual external energy to
maintain, and creates flood/erosion problems that didn't exist before.

When you measure flooding in modern wetland regions and compare it
to historical flooding, the apparent increase is partly:

- Real precipitation change (small)
- Loss of natural infiltration (large)
- **Plus the secondary effect of paved surfaces directing water
  toward developed areas that were previously bypassed**

The technology-pinnacle assumption excludes this third factor from
"flood" framing entirely.

## Variables in the Flood Matrix

For each flood event, attempt to populate:

### Hydrology variables (physics)

- `precipitation_total_mm`
- `precipitation_rate_mm_per_hour` (peak intensity)
- `antecedent_soil_moisture` (only post-2015 satellite)
- `peak_streamflow_m3s` (gauge-dependent)
- `peak_stage_m` (gauge water level)
- `flow_duration_above_bankfull_hours`
- `total_runoff_volume_m3` (modeled)

### Land-use variables (CRITICAL -- usually missing)

- `watershed_impervious_fraction`
- `watershed_wetland_fraction`
- `riparian_buffer_width_m`
- `channel_modification_status` (natural / channelized / leveed)
- `floodplain_development_density`
- `upstream_dam_capacity_m3`

### Damage variables (NOT physics -- exposure)

- `economic_damage_USD` (inflation + valuation method)
- `structures_affected` (depends on what's there)
- `population_displaced`
- `FEMA_declaration` (political event)
- `insurance_claims_filed` (insurance penetration changes)

### Methodology versioning

- `streamgage_network_density_at_event`
- `FIRM_version_at_event`
- `flood_frequency_calculation_method`
- `bulletin_17_version` (Bulletin 17B vs 17C -- different statistics)

## Specific flood claims that fail audit

### Claim: "100-year floods are now 10-year floods"

- **Audit findings:**
  1. "100-year flood" defined by historical record (typically
     1945-1995) on watersheds that were already 50%+ developed
  2. Modern recurrence calculated on watersheds 80%+ developed
  3. Comparing two different landscapes, calling the difference
     "climate"
- **Honest restatement:** "Peak flows that historically occurred
  every 100 years on partially-developed watersheds now occur
  every 10 years on heavily-developed watersheds. This reflects
  some combination of precipitation trend (small), land-use
  change (large), and methodology change (medium)."

### Claim: "Flood damage is increasing exponentially"

- **Audit findings:**
  1. Damage measured in inflation-adjusted dollars
  2. Floodplain development increased dramatically over period
  3. National Flood Insurance Program subsidized rebuilding
  4. Population in flood zones tripled 1950-2020
- **Honest restatement:** "Flood damage in dollar terms is
  increasing because we have placed more dollar-denominated
  property in flood zones. Per-capita damage and per-structure
  damage trends are much weaker."

### Claim: "Climate change is making floods worse"

- **Audit findings:**
  1. Some regions show real precipitation extremes increase
  2. Land-use change effect is larger in most basins
  3. The two are not separated in most attribution studies
  4. Pre-settlement baselines are not used (would show modern
     "floods" in larger context)
- **Honest restatement:** "Climate change appears to be
  contributing to increased precipitation extremes in some
  regions. The contribution to observed flood frequency and
  damage is smaller than the contribution from land-use change
  and floodplain development. Most attribution studies do not
  separate these effects."

## Surrogate calibration sources to build

### Curve 1: Land-use-corrected flood frequency

- Modern reference: paired watersheds (developed vs undeveloped)
  with similar precipitation
- Build flow-amplification factor as function of impervious surface
- Apply: deflate modern flood records to natural-landscape equivalent

### Curve 2: Pre-settlement flood baseline

- Sources: paleoflood deposits, oxbow lake sediments, tree rings
- Build distribution of "natural" flood frequency on intact landscapes
- Apply: contextualize modern floods against pre-engineered baseline

### Curve 3: Channelization correction

- Modern reference: pre/post-channelization gauge records
  (where available -- Mississippi, Missouri, etc.)
- Build peak-flow amplification factor for channelized streams
- Apply: estimate natural-channel equivalent flows

### Curve 4: FEMA-declaration vs physical-flood

- Modern reference: FEMA disasters with concurrent gauge data
- Build calibration showing political vs physical thresholds
- Apply: deflate FEMA-declaration "trends" to physical-flood trends

## Specific cautionary notes for analysts

- **Never use FEMA disaster declarations as a physical flood
  metric.** They are political/administrative events.
- **Never compare flood frequency across decades without
  documenting watershed development changes.** A "100-year flood"
  on a 1950 watershed is physically different from a "100-year
  flood" on a 2020 watershed.
- **Bulletin 17B vs 17C give different recurrence intervals.**
  USGS updated the standard in 2019. Pre-2019 and post-2019
  flood frequency claims use different statistics.
- **Stream gauge network is shrinking.** Any "increasing flood
  frequency" claim must account for whether new floods are
  being detected by new gauges or whether the same gauges are
  recording more events.
- **Pre-1990 Bulletin 17B used log-Pearson Type III only.**
  Bulletin 17C (2019+) uses station-skew weighting and improved
  outlier handling. Old "100-year flood" thresholds calculated
  under 17B are not directly comparable to 17C values.
- **Wetland loss is the largest single flood-buffering change
  in the past 200 years.** Any flood trend analysis that doesn't
  include wetland-extent time series is incomplete.

## The framework problem itself

This is the section that distinguishes flood metrology from the
other domains audited.

**The flood literature does not have a methodology problem.
It has an ASSUMPTION problem.**

The standard frame:

> "How is climate change affecting flood frequency and severity?"

This frame embeds the assumption that:

- Floods are caused by precipitation
- Therefore changes in flood frequency reflect changes in
  precipitation
- Therefore changes in flood frequency reflect climate

This frame **excludes by construction** the possibility that:

- Floods are caused by water meeting impervious surface
- Therefore changes in flood frequency reflect changes in
  surface hydrology
- Therefore changes in flood frequency reflect land-use choice

Both frames are physically valid. The institutional literature
chose the first. Data collected within that frame **cannot test
the second hypothesis**, because the variables needed (impervious
surface fraction, wetland loss rate, channel modification status)
are not in the database.

A proper flood metrology audit would require:

1. Adding land-use variables to flood event records
2. Re-running historical attribution with land-use as candidate cause
3. Acknowledging that pre-1900 data cannot validate post-2000
   trends because the LANDSCAPE changed, not just the climate
4. Stating uncertainty bounds that include framework choice

This is the deepest finding of the entire metrology audit project:

> **The corruption is not just in measurement methodology.
> It is in the FRAMEWORK that decides which measurements
> are relevant.**

The operational tool that emerges from this finding is
`assumption_bias_detector.py`, which generalizes the framework
audit across all six domains in this registry collection.
