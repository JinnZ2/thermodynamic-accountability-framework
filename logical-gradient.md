Principles of Logical Gradient: Claim → Theorem → Axiom

Preamble

This document defines the criteria by which a logical claim ascends the logical gradient within the thermodynamic accountability framework. It treats logical claims not as abstract truths that float free of substrate, but as low-entropy constraints on possible configurations. A logical structure that holds across all tested media without exception has the status of an invariant. The gradient measures the increasing stability of that invariant under pressure.

The gradient has four stages: Logical Claim, Valid Inference, Sound Argument / Theorem, and Axiomatic Law. Each stage demands a specific type and cost of testing, a defined falsifiability window, and an invariant that must hold across translation between media.

---

1. Logical Claim

Definition: A proposed relationship between statements that would constrain the space of possible configurations if true. It has not yet survived structured testing.

Entry requirements:

· A clear statement of the claimed logical relationship (if P then Q, P and not-Q is impossible, etc.).
· A declared accounting medium: the domain of discourse in which the claim is meant to operate (e.g., physical objects, financial accounts, ethical obligations, formal symbols).
· At least one falsifiable condition: a specific configuration that, if observed or constructed, would break the claimed relationship within its medium.
· Energy cost of articulation recorded.

Status: Speculative. May guide attention but not action.

Thermodynamic gate: None—no return measured.

---

2. Valid Inference

Definition: A logical claim that has been tested for structural correctness: the form of reasoning preserves the claimed constraint, regardless of the truth of its premises. No internal counterexample exists in the abstract structure.

Upgrade requirements:

· Formal representation: the inference pattern must be stated in a medium that allows mechanical checking (symbolic logic, code, a diagrammatic calculus, or any explicit rule set).
· A complete search for counterexamples within the formal space must be attempted, or a proof of structural soundness given. If a counterexample is found, the claim fails.
· The inference must be shown to be medium-invariant for its form: the same structure must hold when instantiated with different atomic terms that satisfy the formal constraints. (Analogy: syllogism form holds regardless of whether terms are sheep, numbers, or obligations.)
· Energy cost of the formalization and checking recorded.

Thermodynamic gate: The checking process must return more structural clarity than it consumed in attention and compute. If the inference is so elaborate that its soundness cannot be verified without parasitic energy drain, it is flagged as "over-specified inference" and remains a logical claim.

---

3. Sound Argument / Theorem

Definition: A valid inference whose premises are themselves true within the declared medium, and which survives attempted falsification when the medium is connected to observation or practice. A theorem, in this framework, is a sound argument that has been shown to be necessary: no possible world that respects the premises can avoid the conclusion.

Upgrade requirements:

· All criteria of Valid Inference met.
· Truth-test of premises: each premise must pass a falsification attempt in its declared medium (e.g., empirical observation, lived experience, operational check). If any premise is unfalsifiable, the argument is relegated to "speculative inference."
· Cross-media translation: the logical relationship must be instantiated in at least two different media (e.g., abstract symbols + physical token counting, or ethical reasoning + biological constraint) and observed to hold identically in both. If the relationship breaks in translation, the theorem is medium-bound (valid only in its original medium).
· Attempted construction of a counterexample: a deliberate, recorded attempt to produce a configuration where premises hold and conclusion fails. This attempt must fail for the theorem to stand.
· Peer audit: a second observer or system applying the same falsification protocol must reach the same conclusion.

Thermodynamic gate: The total energy cost of testing premises, translating across media, and searching for counterexamples must be less than the expected cost of errors avoided by using the theorem as a constraint. If not, the theorem is "over-certified" and its application is not thermodynamically justified.

---

4. Axiomatic Law

Definition: A theorem that has never been falsified in any medium, that cannot be reduced to a simpler invariant, and that serves as a necessary constraint for coherent operation across multiple systems. An axiomatic law is not assumed true; it is the surviving invariant after exhaustive pressure.

Upgrade requirements:

· All criteria of Sound Argument / Theorem met.
· Zero unexplained falsification events anywhere in the historical record. A single verified counterexample demotes the law to theorem and forces re-audit.
· Minimum invariance domain: The law must have been tested in at least three fundamentally different media (e.g., physical dynamics, economic exchange, biological signaling, machine logic) and held without exception.
· Predictive power: The law must have correctly predicted the impossibility of certain configurations before they were attempted (e.g., "you cannot construct a perpetual motion machine under these constraints" — predicted, then never falsified).
· Irreducibility: No simpler invariant (by minimum description length) can capture the same constraint. If a simpler invariant is found, the original law is demoted to a derivative case.
· Temporal survival: The law must have remained unfalsified for a period long enough that the probability of an undetected counterexample is below a defined threshold, with continuous monitoring.

Thermodynamic gate: The law must demonstrate net positive thermodynamic return across all systems that deploy it as a constraint. That is, systems using the law as a hard boundary must save more energy (or generate less waste) than the total energy ever spent to test, formalize, and audit the law. This gate ensures that axiomatic status is not a philosophical trophy but an engineering certification.

---

The Role of Gradients

Each stage is reached by climbing a steeper falsification surface:

· Claim → Valid Inference: formal structural soundness.
· Valid Inference → Theorem: truth of premises + cross-media invariance.
· Theorem → Axiomatic Law: historical survival, predictive novelty, irreducibility, and thermodynamic return.

The framework does not require that all logical claims become axioms. It requires that the energy spent to elevate a claim be accounted for, and that no claim be held as axiom under parasitic load (i.e., when the cost of maintaining the axiom exceeds the cost of errors it prevents).
