Principles of Epistemic Gradient: Hypothetical → Theory → Law

Preamble

This document defines the criteria by which a claim ascends the epistemic gradient within the thermodynamic accountability framework. It does not depend on narrative authority, credential, or institutional consensus. It depends on falsifiable pattern stability under increasing thermodynamic pressure.

The gradient has four stages: Hypothetical, Robust Hypothesis, Theory, and Law. Each stage demands a specific type and cost of testing, a defined falsifiability window, and an invariant that must hold across media.

---

1. Hypothetical

Definition: A proposed pattern that would explain or predict a set of observations, but has not yet survived any falsification attempt beyond its initial articulation.

Entry requirements:

· A clear, medium-specific statement of the pattern.
· A declared invariant (what must hold for the pattern to be considered the same).
· At least one falsifiable condition: "If X is observed in medium Y, the hypothetical is disproven."
· Energy cost of articulation is recorded (optional but recommended).

Status: Speculative. Does not guide action without explicit uncertainty declaration.

Thermodynamic gate: None—no return measured.

---

2. Robust Hypothesis

Definition: A hypothetical that has survived a specified set of falsification attempts and has been observed to hold under varied conditions, but not yet across fundamentally different accounting media.

Upgrade requirements:

· At least three independent tests, each designed to falsify the claim under different conditions.
· Tests must be run in the same declared accounting medium as the hypothetical (e.g., if the claim is about joules, tests must measure joules).
· A public record of each test, including raw data and outcome.
· No unexplained failure (a single genuine falsification event demotes the hypothesis back to hypothetical, or kills it).
· A measured signal-to-noise ratio: the pattern must be distinguishable from random variation at a specified confidence level (e.g., 5σ if statistical, or its qualitative equivalent in non-statistical media).

Thermodynamic gate: Applied to the testing process itself—did the tests return more falsification pressure than they consumed? If not, testing was parasitic and the hypothesis remains ungraded.

---

3. Theory

Definition: A robust hypothesis that has survived cross-media translation—i.e., the same invariant holds when tested in at least two fundamentally different accounting media (e.g., joules and labor-hours, or attention and material flow). A theory is not a final truth, but it is stable enough to build systems on.

Upgrade requirements:

· All criteria of Robust Hypothesis met.
· At least two media-translation tests: the invariant must be formulated in the original medium, and then the same pattern must be observed when measured in a second, non-overlapping medium, with no extra free parameters.
· A documented "minimum ontological core"—the minimal set of relationships that must persist across media for the theory to survive translation. If any element of the core fails in a new medium, the theory is downgraded to robust hypothesis (medium-specific).
· A cross-medium falsification attempt: a deliberate attempt to break the invariant by changing the measurement substrate. This must fail (i.e., the invariant holds) for the theory to hold.
· Peer audit by at least one independent observer using the same audit protocol (the observer need not be human; an AI or a sensor network can serve as auditor if it can run falsification tests).

Thermodynamic gate: The total energy cost of all cross-media tests must be less than the thermodynamic return of the theory's application (if applied) over a specified time horizon, or the theory is flagged as "over-priced" and not yet ready for law status.

---

4. Law

Definition: A theory that has held invariant across all tested media, has never been falsified in any recorded instance, has been used to successfully predict novel phenomena in at least one medium not originally tested, and has no surviving rival theory that explains the same observations with equal or lesser thermodynamic cost.

Upgrade requirements:

· All criteria of Theory met.
· Zero unexplained falsification events in the historical record. (A single verified falsification event that cannot be attributed to measurement error demotes the law to theory and triggers immediate re-audit.)
· Novel prediction confirmed: the law must have predicted a pattern before it was observed, in a medium not used in the theory's development. The prediction must be recorded timestamped prior to observation.
· Minimum survival period: The law must have persisted without falsification for a duration long enough that the probability of a hidden falsification event is below a defined threshold (e.g., 5% probability of hidden failure over 10-year horizon, with continuous monitoring).
· Parsimony: No simpler invariant (measured by minimum description length) can explain the same set of observations. If a simpler invariant emerges, the original law may be reduced to a special case of the new one.
· Transparency: All data, methods, and falsification attempts are publicly available in perpetuity.

Thermodynamic gate: The law must demonstrate net positive return when used as an operational constraint in real systems: the energy saved (or entropy reduced) by relying on the law must exceed the total energy spent to test it over its lifespan.

---

The Role of "Gradients"

The term gradient in this document refers to the steepness of the falsification surface a claim must climb to reach the next level:

· From Hypothetical to Robust Hypothesis: gradient is reproducibility within a single medium.
· From Robust Hypothesis to Theory: gradient is cross-media invariance.
· From Theory to Law: gradient is predictive novelty and total historical survival.

Each gradient has a cost. The framework does not require that all claims be pushed to law; it requires that the cost of pushing be transparently weighed against the thermodynamic return. Most claims will remain robust hypotheses or theories, and that is correct.

---

Falsifiability and the Thermodynamic Gate

At every stage, the claim must be falsifiable. If no conceivable observation could disprove the claim in its declared medium, it cannot enter the gradient at all—it is narrative, not hypothesis.

The thermodynamic gate is applied at each upgrade: if the testing process consumes more than it returns (in energy, attention, or the declared accounting medium), the upgrade is invalid until the cost is recovered. This prevents the accumulation of untested, parasitic narrative under the name of science.
