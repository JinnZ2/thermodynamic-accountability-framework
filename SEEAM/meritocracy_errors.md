1. Claim: “Outcomes are determined by individual talent and effort, independent of resource allocation.”

What it assumes: That talent and effort alone produce success, ignoring the massive energy input required to convert potential into a degree, a job, or a career.

Analogy:
A barrel of crude oil is “successful” because it refined itself into gasoline and then sold itself at a profit. The barrel had “grit,” so it deserved to become fuel. In reality, the barrel paid nothing for the drilling rig, the pipeline, or the refinery—all were funded by investors who captured the value. If the barrel were required to pay for its own extraction, it would be deeply in debt before it ever reached the market.

The dependence that’s hidden:
Education, healthcare, nutrition, stable housing, social capital—these are the “infrastructure” that converts potential into output. In a meritocratic narrative, these are often framed as personal choices or family generosity, not as systemic pre-conditions.

Fallacy: Reification + False Cause

· Reification: Treating “merit” as a concrete property of the individual, as if it exists in isolation from the resource flows that activate it.
· False Cause (post hoc ergo propter hoc): Observing that successful people had talent and effort, then concluding talent and effort alone caused the success, ignoring the resource pipeline that was required to express them.

2. Claim: “The market rewards individuals according to the value they create; inequality is therefore a fair reflection of merit.”

What it assumes: That compensation equals productivity, and that productivity is an intrinsic trait of the worker, not a function of the capital they have access to.

Analogy:
A tree in a plantation is sold for timber at a high price. The plantation owner claims: “This tree is more valuable than the one in the wild because it’s a better tree.” In truth, the plantation tree was irrigated, pruned, fertilized, and protected from pests—all investments paid for by the owner, often by extracting wealth from other trees (or future harvests). The wild tree had the same potential but lacked the resource pipeline. If trees could talk, the plantation tree would boast about its “work ethic,” unaware that its success was built on the starvation of the wild grove.

The dependence that’s hidden:
Capital, infrastructure, and labor conditions are provided by the firm or society. The worker’s output is the product of this machinery, yet wages are negotiated as if the worker personally owns the refinery.

Fallacy: Begging the Question (Circular Reasoning)

· The market defines what value is, and then the market rewards those who create it. “Value” is whatever the market pays for. The claim that the market rewards value creation is thus true by definition—but only if you accept that the market’s price is the correct measure of contribution. The argument’s conclusion is baked into its premise.

3. Claim: “Education is a great equalizer; investing in human capital lets talent rise.”

What it assumes: That the primary barrier to success is lack of skill, and that providing education will naturally lift the poor without harming others.

Analogy:
A farmer diverts all water from a shared river to irrigate a single field. That field produces an abundant crop. The farmer then claims: “See? Water works! We should invest in more water for that field.” The downstream fields dry up, but the farmer points to the single green patch as proof that the system works. The success of the irrigated field is the cause of the others’ failure, not evidence that water is a universal equalizer.

The dependence that’s hidden:
When resources (loans, parental time, public subsidies) are concentrated on one child, the remaining children are systematically deprived. The “success” of the educated child is the thermodynamic cost paid by the siblings.

Fallacy: Fallacy of Composition

· What is true for one individual (education improved their outcome) is assumed to be true for the whole group (educating everyone would improve total outcomes without tradeoffs). But in a finite-resource system, concentrating resources on some necessarily diverts them from others. The aggregate may not increase; it may decline if the extraction node drains more than it returns.

4. Claim: “If you work hard, you can pull yourself up by your bootstraps.”

What it assumes: That the individual has no dependence on external support, and that effort alone can overcome structural barriers.

Analogy:
A spaceship claims it reached orbit by pulling on its own hull. The captain says, “I grabbed the tail and pulled myself up.” In reality, the spacecraft required a massive external rocket, fuel, and ground control—all provided by a society that paid for it. The bootstrap metaphor is physically impossible: to lift yourself, you need a reaction mass to push against, and you can’t push against yourself. In thermodynamics, this is a violation of momentum conservation.

The dependence that’s hidden:
The “bootstrap” is actually the shoulders of others: parents, public schools, roads, legal systems, and a stable economy. The narrative erases these dependencies to make the success appear self-generated.

Fallacy: Non Sequitur / Denying the Antecedent

· “If you work hard, you will succeed. Therefore, if you didn’t succeed, you didn’t work hard.” This ignores all the other necessary conditions (resources, luck, structural access). The presence of hard work is mistaken for the sole sufficient condition.

5. Claim: “Meritocracy is fair because everyone has equal opportunity to compete.”

What it assumes: That the starting line is the same for all, and that the race is run on a level track.

Analogy:
A race is announced where the prize is a full college scholarship. All children are invited to the starting line, but only those whose parents can afford special running shoes, private coaches, and a diet plan actually show up fit. The others arrive barefoot, having walked miles to the track. The winner declares, “I won because I’m the fastest runner.” The shoes, coaching, and nutrition—paid for by parental wealth—are invisible to the meritocratic eye. The competition was fair in name only.

The dependence that’s hidden:
The “opportunity” is not a vacuum; it is a resource-intensive infrastructure. Equal opportunity would require equal access to that infrastructure. Without it, the competition measures not talent but resource concentration.

Fallacy: Formal Fallacy (Ignoring the Consequent)

· “If the system is fair, then outcomes will reflect talent. Outcomes reflect talent; therefore, the system is fair.” This affirms the consequent. Talent could also be expressed under an unfair system that happens to sort people similarly. The fairness of the system is not proven by the existence of talented winners.

6. Claim: “Those who succeed owe nothing to those who do not; their wealth is their own.”

What it assumes: That success is a closed-loop transaction between the individual and the market, with no unaccounted externalities.

Analogy:
A factory owner boasts that his profits are entirely his own, because he bought the raw materials and paid the workers. But his factory chimney spews toxic smoke over the neighboring town, causing health costs and lost productivity. Those costs—the drag—are borne by the townsfolk, not by the owner. The profit is only “earned” if you ignore the smoke. Similarly, the debt-ridden sibling, the depressed non-graduate, the childless couple who couldn’t afford housing—these are the “smoke” of the prized node’s success.

The dependence that’s hidden:
Every success in a closed system extracts an entropy cost from the rest of the system. The prized node’s degree was paid for by the siblings’ reduced opportunities and the parents’ depleted retirement. To claim sole ownership of the output is to deny the thermodynamic bookkeeping.

Fallacy: Special Pleading (ignoring the counter-evidence)

· Applying a standard to the successful (their effort caused their wealth) while exempting them from the systemic costs that made it possible. The successful are credited with the gains, but the losses are treated as unrelated coincidences.

7. Meta-Claim: “Meritocracy is not a perfect system, but it’s the best we have.”

What it assumes: That the alternative is worse, and that the current system’s “imperfections” are random noise rather than systematic extraction.

Analogy:
A casino claims that its roulette wheel is “imperfect” because the house edge is small, and it’s the best game in town. But the wheel is magnetized: certain numbers are subtly weighted to lose more often, and the players who win are celebrated as proof that anyone can win. The “imperfections” are actually a designed feature that transfers wealth from the many to the few. The casino is not “imperfect”; it’s rigged. The narrative of imperfection protects the rigging from scrutiny.

The dependence that’s hidden:
The “imperfections” are the resource shunts, the entropic propagation, and the self-financing burden. They are not noise; they are the mechanism by which the system maintains itself.

Fallacy: Appeal to Consequences / Status Quo Bias

· “We must believe in meritocracy because the alternative (acknowledging systemic extraction) would lead to social unrest or despair.” The truth of a claim is not determined by the desirability of its consequences. Moreover, assuming the current system is the best possible alternative is a form of status quo bias—it assumes, without evidence, that no better system exists.
